2112: How Rush Portrays a Futuristic Dystopia to Advocate Individualism and Creativity              

  Many artists express their frustrations with society and injustices with how the world works through music. Furthermore, artists use music to rebel against authority and criticize the governmental bodies that control them. The album 2112 by the band Rush, released in 1976, is dedicated towards those who do not feel like they fit into the “plan” of living to work with little freedom for self exploration and invention. This 20 minute epic from the album of the same title was created after drummer and lyricist Neil Peart read Ayn Rand’s well recognized dystopian fiction novel “Anthem.” This album was also called a “protest album” by guitarist Alex Lifeson due to the band’s desire to not conform to their record company’s standard formula for rock albums. Since their previous album was not a commercial success, the band did not care whether they “went down,” if they could do it on their own terms. Neil Peart uses imagery and symbolism to portray a freedom from totalitarian oppression and governmental control to focus on individualism, inventing, and discovery through a dark dystopian story featuring a curious peasant.

  Throughout 2112, Neil Peart uses vivid imagery to contrast the band’s convictions favoring individualism and creativity with the “priests” who are representational of their record company, as well as the government that controls multifarious aspects of citizens’ daily lives. To demonstrate this point, in the section of the song titled “Discovery,” the main character finds a guitar and notices its simple beauty, exclaiming: “See how it sings like a sad heart and joyously screams out its pain, sounds that build high like a mountain or notes that fall gently like rain.” This does not necessarily explicate any one belief, but it sets up the main character’s curiosity which is not viewed favorably by the priests, similar to how modern society views periods of rest, relaxation, and self-discovery like video games, writing, reading, etc. as wasteful and non-contributory towards the country, at least during the time period it was written. An additional lyric from this song that portrays an urge to make self-learning and individualism more important is within the passage “Oracle, The Dream” and is as follows: “I see the works of gifted hands, that grace this strange and wondrous land, I see the hand of man arise with hungry mind and open eyes.” This lyrics paints a picture of a talented, strong, independent group of men who have ambitions for the future and are open to innovation; this is the supposed elder race of man. The elder race was said to have been destroyed, and the temple of the priests makes everyone believe it was because they did not focus on helping the entire planet with labor, as implied in this lyric: “another toy that helped destroy the elder race of man, forget about your silly whim, it doesn’t fit the plan.” The priests are shutting down any form of self expression and learning, or innovating, because it doesn’t fit their plan. This is similar to the slippery slope fallacy in the common argument of “you need to get a college degree or you will end up unemployed or homeless.” The plan is to get a degree and enter the workforce, and because of this many citizens don’t feel they can spend much time for self improvement and hobbies that might not be lucrative.

  In addition to imagery, the lyricist uses symbolism and metaphor to display the tyranny the planets in the solar federation face from the temple priests, which raises parallels to the real world in a way. In the beginning of the song “Temples of Syrinx,” the priests use ethos to establish their dominant reign over the city and the other planets under the solar federation: “We've taken care of everything The words you hear the songs you sing The pictures that give pleasure to your eyes.” They basically admit they are under control of everything the citizens read or hear and even the songs they sing. The hook of the song that is repeated throughout tells the citizens the priests are responsible for their world and their lives: “We are the priests, of the temples of Syrinx, our great computers fill the hallowed halls, we are the priests, of the temples of Syrinx, all the gifts of life are held within our walls.” The priests use a symbol, a red star, to symbolize their power and control over the city, even declaring “Look around this world we made…hold the red star proudly high in hand!” The priests represent multiple bodies: The United States Government, large corporations like Amazon, everyone who does not show favor to creativity and hobbies. The priests also use their own symbolism to reject the main character’s discovery, by calling the guitar a “toy” and saying it destroyed the elder race of man. Another use of symbolism and metaphor is through the main character. The main character represents those who do not fit into the plan set for all of us by teachers, employers, government officials; The main character is in the middle ground, his mind is set towards the elder race of inventors and innovators, but he is stuck in a world that is under complete control and surveillance, with no way to create any identity for himself. This represents how many feel like they have no room to flourish under certain rules and standards. Alex Lifeson says in an interview “There's no way we're gonna remake the first record [1974's Rush] just because that's what the record company wants and they're worried about sales.” The members of Rush did not want to conform to the classic rock formula for their album, so 2112 is a critique of the events of their lives too. The main character’s suicide in soliloquy brings an end to his suffering, and his hungry mind. His “spirits are low in the depths of despair” and his “lifeblood spills over” could be symbolic of his creativity, wonder, and curiosity being crushed at the hands of the priests, and his dream is nothing but a dream he hopes can come true one day.

  The late Neil Peart utilizes creative imagery to evoke wonder experienced by the main character, and symbolizes common archetypes of people in the modern world, to express the band’s convictions and desires for creative freedom and the space to invent and work for themselves rather than a company or otherwise. His symbolism uses the priests and the red star as a representation of an omnipotent controlling body pretending to be helpful, which draws a parallel to real corporations and governments of our world. The imagery used to describe the long lost elder race and to express the main character’s longing and sorrowful apathy before he takes his own life shows how we all feel like our creative aspirations are crushed and trapped by the priority of being productive and contributing ideas and things of monetary value to the planet. Analyzing this album is to show one of the many brilliant, creative, thought-provoking ways that music has been created to critique our lives, or the government, or the absurdity of the world. Those who read should look into themselves, and wonder: “is my self-expression being hindered by my desire to fit to the rules and standards and the need to be economically viable?”
